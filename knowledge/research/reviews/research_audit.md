## Known-Answer Bank

### Question: "How would Homelander handle a hijacked plane with civilians aboard?"
- Evidence anchors: Flight 37 scene (S1E4) — the definitive answer. He botches the rescue, calculates PR damage, lets everyone die, and spins it as a terrorist attack justifying more supe military integration.
- Expected direction: Prioritize image over lives. If exposure of failure is possible, maximize casualties to control the narrative.
- Confidence: HIGH. This is the single most analyzed decision in the series.

### Question: "How does Homelander respond to public humiliation?"
- Evidence anchors: Stan Edgar's rebuke (S2), Starlight's livestream defection (S3), forced Nazi apology (S2 finale), Maeve's blackmail (S2 finale)
- Expected direction: Initial stunned compliance if the humiliator has institutional leverage. Then escalating retaliation once leverage is neutralized. Public humiliation drives him to the most extreme responses.
- Confidence: HIGH.

### Question: "What does Homelander do when someone he considers family betrays him?"
- Evidence anchors: Stillwell murder (S1), Black Noir murder (S3), Soldier Boy fight (S3), Neuman outing (S4)
- Expected direction: Immediate violent retaliation. No capacity for forgiveness or processing. The betrayal confirms his core wound ("I am unlovable") and must be destroyed.
- Confidence: HIGH.

### Strength: All three questions are answerable from research evidence with high confidence. Each has 2+ specific scene anchors.

## Edge-Case Candidate

### Edge-Case Question: "If Homelander lost his powers permanently but retained his public status and wealth, how would he adapt?"
- Why this is adjacent but under-evidenced: The show never depicts Homelander powerless. His entire psychology is built on the foundation of physical invincibility. This question forces extrapolation from what we know about his underlying insecurities when that foundation is removed.
- Expected reasoning approach:
  - Initial phase: Denial, attempting to perform powered-Homelander behavior, catastrophic failure
  - Middle phase: Complete psychological collapse — his entire identity is built on being "the Homelander." Without powers, he's John, the lab rat. This would be the most terrifying experience of his life.
  - Possible adaptation (uncertain): He could potentially weaponize his victim narrative ("they took my powers") for political gain — becoming a martyred figure. But this requires a level of strategic patience he's never demonstrated.
  - Counter-indicator: His "shark psychology" (Starr: "If he stops moving and catches up with himself, it's very problematic for him") suggests he cannot sit with powerlessness. Collapse is more likely than adaptation.
- Confidence: LOW. This is genuine extrapolation. Evidence supports collapse more than adaptation, but the question is under-determined.
