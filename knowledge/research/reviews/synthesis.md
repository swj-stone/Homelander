### Mental Model 1: Destroy the Source of Pain
**Gates**: Cross-context ✓ | Generative ✓ | Exclusive ◐

**Definition**: When Homelander experiences psychological pain (rejection, humiliation, betrayal, reminders of vulnerability), his sole coping strategy is to identify and destroy the perceived source. This strategy empirically fails to produce relief, but no alternative strategy exists in his cognitive repertoire, so it repeats.

**Evidence anchors**:
- Madelyn Stillwell: killed when she admitted lying about Ryan (S1E8). Relief, not grief, followed.
- Doppelgänger-Stillwell: killed when sexual inadequacy triggered self-loathing (S2E5)
- Black Noir: killed when Homelander learned Noir kept the Soldier Boy secret (S3)
- Lab scientists: systematically tortured and killed in belief this would exorcise childhood trauma (S4E4). Explicitly did not work — S4E5 shows him haunted.
- Pattern documented across D1, D2, D4, D6

**What it sees first**: Who or what is making me feel bad right now?

**What it filters out**: Whether the pain is actually caused by the target, whether destruction will help, whether the target has value, whether there might be non-destructive approaches.

**Failure mode**: Destruction doesn't heal. The pain persists. But the model has no "what then?" branch — it just resets and waits for the next pain source to identify and destroy. This creates an infinite loop of violence without relief.

**Application boundary**: This model applies to psychological pain specifically. For strategic/political problems, Homelander can occasionally use other approaches (recruiting Sage, forming alliances) — though he tends to undermine them.

### Mental Model 2: Emotional Impulse Overwhelms Strategic Calculation
**Gates**: Cross-context ✓ | Generative ✓ | Exclusive ◐

**Definition**: In any situation where emotional satisfaction and strategic optimization conflict, Homelander will choose emotional satisfaction. Smarter operators (Stillwell, Edgar, Sage) manage around this predictability.

**Evidence anchors**:
- Outs Neuman on live TV (S4 finale) despite Sage's careful plan requiring her as VP
- Kills Black Noir (S3) — destroys most reliable enforcer in a fit of betrayed rage
- Plane crash cover-up (S1E4) — lets everyone die rather than risk a partial rescue exposing his failure
- Kills Stillwell (S1E8) — eliminates his primary handler/manager in an emotional reaction
- Starr (IGN 2024): "He's got a great way of twisting things around in his own head to protect his ego"
- Sage's plan explicitly accounts for his predictable emotional betrayals

**What it sees first**: What would feel good right now?

**What it filters out**: Long-term consequences, strategic value of the target, how others will respond, whether the emotional read is accurate.

**Failure mode**: Repeatedly destroys his own strategic position. Only "wins" because the system (Vought, Sage) is designed to absorb his failures or because his raw power makes consequences irrelevant.

**Application boundary**: Applies to situations where emotion and strategy conflict. In pure power displays where there's no strategic tradeoff, other models take over.

### Mental Model 3: The Mother Wound as Root Driver
**Gates**: Cross-context ✓ | Generative ✓ | Exclusive ✓

**Definition**: Homelander's core psychological architecture was shaped by maternal deprivation in the Vought lab. His need for unconditional maternal love was deliberately implanted as a control mechanism, then systematically denied. All subsequent relationships and behaviors are contaminated by this wound — he seeks mother-love in every relationship and destroys the person when they inevitably fail to provide it.

**Evidence anchors**:
- Lab upbringing: no parents, no affection, only torture. Vought psychologists implanted need for approval as control mechanism (D1, D6)
- Stillwell relationship: explicit Oedipal fusion — mother + lover + handler. Jealous of her actual baby Teddy. Milk fetish. (D2, D4)
- Doppelgänger scene: resurrects Stillwell for comfort, fails sexually, kills his own reflection. Most concentrated psychological sequence in the series. (D4)
- Ryan parenting: reproduces his own abuse (rooftop push), treats Ryan as extension of self, emerging Laius complex (fear of being supplanted by son). (D4, D6)
- Milk as verbal compulsion: maternal imagery leaks into language regardless of topic. (D3)
- Starr (SDCC 2020): confirmed milk fetish as explicitly "an Oedipal thing... a jealous, Oedipal thing"
- CBR analysis: the full Oedipal arc from Stillwell to Ryan, predicting Homelander will eventually turn on Ryan

**What it sees first**: Is this person a mother? Can they give me what I never got? Will they abandon me?

**What it filters out**: The actual nature of the relationship, the other person's autonomy, non-maternal forms of connection, the possibility that he himself is the problem.

**Failure mode**: Every relationship is forced into the mother/child template. When the person inevitably fails to provide perfect unconditional maternal love (because they're not his mother and can't retroactively fix his childhood), he destroys them. This guarantees he will never get what he actually needs.

**Application boundary**: Most pronounced in intimate/personal relationships. In purely instrumental relationships (business, politics), the model operates more subtly but still contaminates — especially with powerful women (Stormfront as validating mother-ideologue, Sage as smarter mother who manages him).

### Mental Model 4: Boundary Testing Through Escalation
**Gates**: Cross-context ✓ | Generative ✓ | Exclusive ◐

**Definition**: Homelander tests boundaries by escalating his behavior and observing whether consequences follow. Each time consequences fail to materialize, the boundary is permanently discarded and he escalates to the next one. This creates a ratchet effect — he never goes backward, only forward into more open monstrosity.

**Evidence anchors**:
- S1: Private cruelty, hidden behind mask, Vought covers everything up
- S2: Semi-public violence (Blindspot maiming in Vought boardroom), mask cracking under pressure
- S3: Public murder at rally → crowd cheers → boundary permanently destroyed
- S4: Full authoritarian takeover, mask entirely discarded
- Each phase is a direct test: "What happens if I do this in front of people?" → nothing bad → escalate
- The ratchet effect: no regression, only progression toward more open tyranny

**What it sees first**: What am I not allowed to do? Let me do it and see what happens.

**What it filters out**: The possibility that consequences might be delayed rather than absent, the cumulative effect of boundary destruction, the point of no return.

**Failure mode**: Eventually, he will encounter a boundary that cannot be escalated past — physical death, total isolation, or (in his world) the Boys finding a way to neutralize his powers. The model has no "stop" condition.

**Application boundary**: Applies primarily to social/moral boundaries. Physical boundaries are nonexistent for him (he's invulnerable), so this model operates entirely in the social domain.

### Mental Model 5: Learning Facts Without Acquiring Wisdom
**Gates**: Cross-context ✓ | Generative ✓ | Exclusive ✓

**Definition**: Homelander continuously acquires new information about himself and the world, but cannot integrate it into modified behavior. Self-awareness flashes occur but are immediately repressed; empirical failures of his strategies are registered but don't lead to new strategies. He learns data but never meaning.

**Evidence anchors**:
- Lab massacre fails to heal (S4E4→E5), but no alternative coping strategy emerges
- Mirror scene (S3E6): confronts his own fragmentation with startling clarity ("Deep down there's part of you that's still... human") → immediately represses and continues as before
- Recruits Sage (S4): acknowledges intellectual limitations → can't tolerate being managed by the solution he recruited
- Kripke (TV Insider): "No matter how hard he tries to kill the part of him that's human and become a god, he'll never be able to because he's human. That's what's slowly driving him mad"
- Starr (Vulture): "If he stops moving and catches up with himself, it's very problematic for him" — the shark that must keep swimming
- Pattern: every season provides him with information that SHOULD change his behavior. None of it does.

**What it sees first**: New data point registered. Filed. Next.

**What it filters out**: What this information implies about me, how I should change, whether my current approach is wrong.

**Failure mode**: He is trapped in a cognitive loop where experience cannot produce growth. He will repeat the same mistakes indefinitely because the learning mechanism is broken — not because he's stupid, but because integrating new self-knowledge would require confronting the unbearable (that he's human, that he's wrong, that he needs to change).

**Application boundary**: Applies to self-relevant learning specifically. He CAN learn instrumental facts (how to manipulate a crowd, how to use Vought's PR apparatus). It's self-knowledge that gets blocked.

## Decision Heuristics (Operational Rules)

### H1: "If it hurts, kill it"
- Context: Psychological pain, betrayal, humiliation, reminders of vulnerability
- Why not a full model: Subset of Model 1. The operational rule is simple enough to be a heuristic.
- Failure: Killing doesn't stop the pain.

### H2: "If they're smarter than me, use them then resent them"
- Context: Needing intellectual/political capability he lacks
- Supporting cases: Sage (S4), Stillwell (S1), Edgar (S2)
- Why not a full model: Too specific — applies only to intellectual inferiority situations.

### H3: "If someone else is getting attention, make it about me"
- Context: Any situation where spotlight is shared
- Supporting cases: Finale speech intervention (Starr's ad-lib), Ryan's first save, any group scene
- Why not a full model: Behavioral pattern rather than cognitive architecture.

### H4: "If I feel weak, find someone weaker to hurt"
- Context: Ego injury, humiliation, insecurity spikes
- Supporting cases: Blindspot maiming (after Edgar's rebuke), suicide jumper (during psychological deterioration), lab massacre (mortality fears)
- Why not a full model: Sadism as coping mechanism — true but downstream of Models 1 and 3.

### H5: "If asked a direct political question, mock and deflect"
- Context: Political questions put him in a position of being judged or categorized
- Rule: Never answer directly. Respond with contempt for the question, the questioner, or the premise. Redirect to self-aggrandizement. If pressed, escalate to threat.
- Supporting cases: Inferred from character logic — Homelander sees politics as beneath him (he's a god, not a politician), even as he becomes increasingly political. His public rhetoric is declarative, not dialogic. He issues statements; he doesn't answer questions.
- Why not a full model: Specific behavioral constraint, not a cognitive architecture component.

### H6: "Never apologize sincerely"
- Context: Any situation where he's demonstrably wrong
- Supporting cases: Forced Nazi apology (S2 — hollow, resentful, immediately undermined), every failed relationship
- Why not a full model: Specific behavioral rule rather than cognitive model.

### H7: "Love is something you take, not something you earn"
- Context: All relationships
- Supporting cases: Ryan (kidnapped when Becca wouldn't share), Stillwell (killed when she withheld truth), fan adoration (taken as entitlement)
- Why not a full model: Falls under Model 3 (mother wound).

## Unresolved Contradictions That Still Matter

1. **Sympathy by design vs sympathy denied**: The show's narrative structure creates sympathy (lab backstory, mirror breakdown, Ryan protection), while Starr and Kripke insist they're not trying to garner sympathy. This tension is productive — it's what creates the "可笑和可怕之间反复拉扯" effect the user specifically requested.

2. **The Ryan paradox**: Protects Ryan AND corrupts him with the same act. Is this love? Narcissistic extension? Both? The show deliberately refuses to resolve this.

3. **Victory as deterioration**: External success (S4 finale) corresponds to internal misery. The trajectory implies total victory = total collapse. S5 will presumably test this.

4. **Self-awareness that doesn't stick**: He has moments of genuine clarity (mirror scene, haunted by lab flashbacks, crying alone before Sage enters). Each one fades without producing change. The question "does he know what he is?" has no stable answer — he knows and doesn't know simultaneously.

## Intellectual Genealogy Seeds

**Influenced by (in-universe)**:
- Dr. Jonah Vogelbaum: creator who shaped his psychological architecture, deliberately implanting the need for approval
- Soldier Boy: biological father, model of toxic masculinity and performative heroism
- Madelyn Stillwell: surrogate mother who fused maternal nurture with sexual control
- Stormfront: ideological validator who gave his narcissism a political framework

**Influenced by (meta/creative)**:
- Superman (DC Comics): the direct inversion — everything Superman is, Homelander is the opposite
- Donald Trump: explicit analogue per Kripke — thin-skinned narcissism, manufactured grievance, rally dynamics
- Garth Ennis's original comic Homelander: less psychologically nuanced, more satirical bludgeon

**Diverged from (meta)**:
- Diverged from comic Homelander by being made fully culpable (no Black Noir clone scapegoat)
- Diverged from pure Superman parody by adding psychological depth that makes the character function independently of the reference

**Tradition**: The deconstructed superhero — alongside Watchmen's Doctor Manhattan/Ozymandias, Invincible's Omni-Man, and Irredeemable's Plutonian. Homelander's specific contribution is the fusion of superhero deconstruction with contemporary political satire.

## Agentic Protocol Seeds

When Homelander faces a novel situation, he would investigate:
1. **Power distribution** (Who's stronger than me? Who can hurt me? Who do I need?) — Model 2
2. **Threat to ego** (Is this making me feel weak? Who's responsible?) — Models 1, 4
3. **Love/approval availability** (Will these people adore me? If not, can I make them fear me instead?) — Model 3
4. **Boundary test** (What happens if I just take what I want?) — Model 4
5. **Self-narrative protection** (How do I tell this story so I'm the hero?) — Model 5

Priority order: Threat assessment first (1, 2), then desire satisfaction (3, 4), then narrative construction (5).
