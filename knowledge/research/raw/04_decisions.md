## Evidence: Major Decisions and Their Logic

### Decision 1: Abandoning Flight 37 (S1E4)
**What happened**: After botching a hijacked plane rescue (he lasers through the cockpit controls), Homelander calculates that saving even a few passengers would expose his failure. He lets everyone die.

**Stated logic**: "They're all dead anyway." There's no PR upside in a partial rescue.

**Revealed logic**: 
- Cannot tolerate public failure in any form
- Values image over lives with zero hesitation
- Processes tragedy exclusively through PR calculus
- No flicker of guilt or moral reasoning — the decision is instantaneous

**Pattern established**: When failure and exposure are the alternatives, he'll choose maximum cruelty every time.

### Decision 2: Killing Stillwell (S1E8)
**What happened**: Homelander murders his handler/surrogate mother after she admits lying about Becca and Ryan.

**Stated logic**: Betrayal punishment. She kept his son from him.

**Revealed logic**:
- The primary attachment figure becomes disposable the moment she's no longer useful
- "Love" for Homelander is entirely contingent on the other person serving his needs
- A pattern: he destroys the people he most depends on (Stillwell, later Black Noir)
- The murder is also liberation — he's been wanting to escape her control

**Pattern established**: Homelander kills what he "loves" when it disappoints him. This will recur with increasing frequency.

### Decision 3: Throwing Ryan Off a Roof (S2)
**What happened**: To "activate" Ryan's powers, Homelander throws his son off a roof. Ryan is injured.

**Stated logic**: "I want him to be strong." He's helping Ryan discover his potential.

**Revealed logic**:
- Cannot distinguish between "helping" and "traumatizing"
- Reproduces his own lab abuse on his son — the cycle is immediate and unconscious
- Sees Ryan as a project/extension, not a person
- Brief, confused remorse when Becca screams at him, but it doesn't lead to change

**Pattern established**: Homelander's parenting is his childhood abuse wearing a "fatherhood" mask. He doesn't know any other way to be.

### Decision 4: The Doppelgänger Murder (S2E5)
**What happened**: After failing to perform sexually with a shapeshifter posing as Stillwell, Homelander has the Doppelgänger transform into himself. He looks at his own face, is momentarily aroused, then kills him.

**Stated logic**: None given. He just does it.

**Revealed logic**:
- Even self-love collapses into self-destruction
- The moment of seeing himself as an object of desire immediately triggers violence
- Sexual inadequacy (with the mother figure) → rage → destruction of the mirror self
- This is the most concentrated moment of his psyche on screen: desire → shame → violence

**Pattern established**: Homelander's sexuality is inextricably fused with maternal need and self-loathing. Any sexual moment risks collapse into violence.

### Decision 5: Killing Black Noir (S3)
**What happened**: Learning Noir knew Soldier Boy was his father and kept it secret, Homelander brutally kills his longest-serving teammate.

**Stated logic**: Betrayal. Noir should have told him.

**Revealed logic**:
- Information asymmetry is experienced as personal attack
- The oldest relationship is not protected — loyalty is only valued in the present tense
- The father revelation destabilizes him so completely that he destroys the messenger
- Destroys a key asset (Noir was his most reliable enforcer) in a fit of emotional reaction

**Pattern established**: Homelander will sacrifice strategic advantage for emotional satisfaction every time. Short-term rage always defeats long-term calculation.

### Decision 6: The Public Murder at the Rally (S3 Finale)
**What happened**: A protester throws a soda can at Ryan. Homelander lasers the man's head off in front of a crowd. After a beat of silence, the crowd erupts in cheers.

**Stated logic**: Protecting his son from an attacker.

**Revealed logic**:
- This is a TEST, conscious or not — "Will they still love me if I do this in public?"
- The answer (yes, they cheer) is transformative. It's the moment the mask becomes optional.
- Ryan smiles. Homelander has just taught his son that murder earns applause.
- This is the most consequential decision he's made: he's discovered that being openly monstrous is safe.

**Pattern established**: Public cruelty + approval = permission to abandon all pretense. This is the Season 3-4 bridge.

### Decision 7: The Lab Massacre (S4E4)
**What happened**: Homelander returns to the underground facility where he was raised, systematically tortures and kills the scientists who abused him, and locks the chief overseer in the isolation room.

**Stated logic**: Revenge. Closure. "Exorcising demons."

**Revealed logic**:
- He believes killing the people who hurt him will kill the hurt itself
- It doesn't work. Episode 5 shows him haunted by flashbacks.
- The revenge is methodical, narrated, almost ceremonial — he wants to be SEEN doing it
- Forces one scientist to masturbate in public (replicating his own childhood humiliation) — a precise mirror of the abuse he suffered
- Kripke's comment: "No matter how hard he tries to kill the part of him that's human and become a god, he'll never be able to because he's human. As much as he's disgusted by it or represses it, he has human emotions. That's what's slowly driving him mad."

**Pattern established**: Homelander's theory of healing is "kill the source of pain." It doesn't work, but he has no other theory to try.

### Decision 8: Choosing Ryan Over Soldier Boy (S3 Finale)
**What happened**: When Soldier Boy attacks Ryan, Homelander fights his own father to protect his son.

**Stated logic**: Ryan is his son. Soldier Boy rejected him.

**Revealed logic**:
- This is his most genuinely selfless decision across four seasons
- But it's also possessive: he protects Ryan while also corrupting him
- The choice is less "good versus evil" and more "my extension versus my rejecter"
- Still: he chose to protect someone else from a direct threat. This is as close to love as Homelander gets.

**Pattern established**: Homelander is capable of protective behavior toward Ryan specifically. Whether this is love or narcissistic extension is deliberately ambiguous.

### Decision 9: Recruiting Sister Sage (S4)
**What happened**: Homelander recognizes the limits of his intelligence and recruits the smartest person alive as his strategist.

**Stated logic**: He needs someone smarter to help him win.

**Revealed logic**:
- A rare moment of genuine self-awareness about his limitations
- But he can't actually tolerate being managed — he chafes at being "a pawn"
- Sage's entire plan accounts for his unpredictability — she plans around his inevitable betrayal of Neuman
- He gains power through her but can't accept that he needed her

**Pattern established**: Homelander can make smart instrumental decisions, but his ego prevents him from fully benefiting from them.

### Decision 10: Outs Victoria Neuman (S4 Finale)
**What happened**: On live television, Homelander impulsively outs Neuman as a Supe, sabotaging Sage's careful plan.

**Stated logic**: Emotional reaction to perceived slights.

**Revealed logic**:
- Cannot tolerate sharing power or credit
- Will destroy a strategic asset for emotional satisfaction
- Sage anticipated this — his predictability is his weakness
- He "wins" anyway because Sage's plan accounted for his failure

**Pattern established**: Homelander consistently makes suboptimal decisions that happen to work out because others (Stillwell, Sage, Neuman) manage around him. His invincibility isn't strategic — it's structural. The system keeps absorbing his mistakes.

## Decision Patterns (Cross-Decision Analysis)

### Pattern A: Emotional Impulse > Strategic Calculation
Every major decision is driven by feeling, not thinking. When there's a conflict between what would be smart and what would feel good, feeling wins. This is consistent across all four seasons.

### Pattern B: Destroy the Source of Pain
His primary problem-solving heuristic: identify what hurts, eliminate it. Applied to: Stillwell (controlling him), Doppelgänger (showing him his reflection), lab scientists (source of trauma), Noir (withheld information). It never works, but he never develops a new approach.

### Pattern C: Test Boundaries Through Escalation
Progression: private cruelty (S1) → semi-public violence (S2) → public murder to see what happens (S3) → full authoritarian takeover (S4). Each season, he tests whether the next level of openness will be punished. It never is.

### Pattern D: The Mother Wound Drives Everything
His maternal deprivation is the root cause of his pathology, and almost every decision can be traced back to it: the Stillwell murder, the milk obsession, the Doppelgänger scene, the need for adoration, the parenting of Ryan.

### Pattern E: Magnificent Tactics, Catastrophic Strategy
Homelander can dominate any immediate situation through intimidation and violence. But he cannot plan, cannot delay gratification, cannot build coalitions that last. He only "wins" because others (Stillwell, Edgar, Sage) manage the system around him.

## Risk Tolerance Profile
- **Physical risk**: Zero. He's invulnerable and knows it. Physical confrontation costs him nothing.
- **Reputational risk**: Initially very low tolerance — the plane cover-up shows image-protection at all costs. By S3-S4, tolerance has risen dramatically as he discovers his base will support anything.
- **Emotional risk**: Extremely low. He cannot tolerate rejection, vulnerability, or being seen as weak. The rare moments of emotional openness (mirror scene, lab confession) are immediately followed by compensatory violence.
- **Strategic risk**: Blind to it. He doesn't think in strategic terms, so he doesn't perceive strategic risk. Others manage it for him.

## Gap Between Stated Values and Revealed Preferences
- **Stated**: "I love America." **Revealed**: Loves being loved by Americans. The country is a mirror.
- **Stated**: "I want to protect people." **Revealed**: Wants to be seen protecting people. Will let them die if failure might be exposed.
- **Stated**: "I'm a father to Ryan." **Revealed**: Treats Ryan as a narcissistic extension and grooming project. Genuine care coexists with instrumental use.
- **Stated**: "I don't need anyone." **Revealed**: Desperately needs everyone's approval and punishes those who withhold it.

---

## Contradictions

1. **The Ryan anomaly**: His only consistent non-selfish behavior is toward Ryan, but even this is contaminated by possessiveness and corruption. Is it love? The evidence is deliberately split.

2. **Intelligence awareness**: He knows he's not the smartest person in the room (he recruited Sage), but can't act on that knowledge consistently. He knows his limitation and ignores it simultaneously.

3. **Self-destruction through self-protection**: Every decision designed to protect his ego (killing Stillwell, alienating Noir, betraying Neuman) ultimately makes his position more precarious. His self-protection instinct is self-destructive.

4. **The lab massacre didn't work**: He believed killing his childhood abusers would free him. It didn't. The show suggests he DOES know this on some level (the haunted flashbacks), but can't process it.

---

## Inferences

1. Homelander would make an excellent case study for "how absolute power corrupts absolutely" specifically because he never had moral formation — the corruption isn't a fall from grace, it's the natural expression of a psyche built without grace.

2. His decision-making is remarkably consistent: identify threat to ego, destroy it, deal with consequences only if forced. This simplicity is what makes him predictable and therefore manageable by smarter operators.

3. The public murder at the S3 rally may be the most important decision in the entire series — it's the moment Homelander learns that the social contract doesn't apply to him, and that there's an audience that will celebrate rather than punish his worst self. Everything in S4 follows from this discovery.
